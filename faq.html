From patients, survivors, and caregivers just like you

During Treatment

Why am I so exhausted during chemotherapy?

You're not imagining it—this exhaustion is real. Almost everyone going through chemotherapy experiences it.

Chemotherapy targets fast-growing cells, including some healthy ones. Your body is working hard to repair this damage while also fighting cancer. This takes enormous energy. On top of that, treatment often disrupts sleep, reduces appetite, and causes anemia (low red blood cells)—all of which add to the fatigue.

What helps:

  • Rest when your body asks for it—this isn't laziness, it's healing
  • Light movement like short walks can actually boost energy
  • Stay hydrated and eat small, frequent meals even when appetite is low
  • Accept help from family and friends; save your energy for what matters most
  • Explore practices that restore prana: breathing exercises, gentle meditation, or energy healing

This fatigue usually improves after treatment ends, but rebuilding your energy takes time and intention.

What can I do about nausea during chemo?

Nausea is one of the most common and distressing side effects. Your body is responding to powerful medicine.

Chemotherapy triggers nausea through multiple pathways—affecting your stomach, your brain's nausea center, and your overall energy balance. When your body's vital energy (*prana*) is disrupted, digestive function often suffers.

What helps:

  • Take anti-nausea medication as prescribed—don't wait for nausea to become severe
  • Eat small, bland meals; avoid strong smells
  • Ginger tea or ginger candies can provide relief
  • Fresh air and gentle breathing exercises help settle the stomach
  • Aromatherapy with peppermint or lemon can ease nausea naturally

I can't sleep during treatment. Is this normal?

Yes, it's very common. Sleep disruption affects most cancer patients during treatment.

Treatment medications, steroids, anxiety, and physical discomfort all interfere with sleep. At an energetic level, your body's natural rhythms have been disrupted. The flow of *prana* that governs rest and recovery needs support to restore balance.

What helps:

  • Maintain a consistent sleep schedule, even when it feels impossible
  • Create a calming bedtime routine—dim lights, no screens
  • Gentle breathing exercises or guided meditation before bed
  • Talk to your doctor about sleep aids if needed
  • Lavender aromatherapy can promote relaxation and better sleep

How do I stay positive when treatment feels endless?

First, know that it's okay not to feel positive all the time. This is hard, and acknowledging that is healthy.

Treatment can feel like an endless tunnel. Your emotional energy—like your physical energy—gets depleted. The constant demands drain your spirit. This is a normal response to an abnormal situation.

What helps:

  • Focus on one day at a time, sometimes one hour at a time
  • Connect with others who understand—support groups make a real difference
  • Allow yourself to feel difficult emotions; suppressing them takes energy
  • Find small moments of joy—a favorite show, a call with a friend
  • Meditation and energy healing can help restore emotional balance

I'm scared I won't complete my treatment because of side effects.

This fear is more common than you might think. You're not alone, and this fear doesn't mean you're weak.

Side effects can be severe enough to make people consider stopping treatment. This is a medical crisis, but it's also an energetic one—your body's vital force is depleted, making everything harder to bear.

What helps:

  • Tell your oncologist about your struggles—doses and schedules can often be adjusted
  • Focus on managing side effects proactively, not just reactively
  • Build a support system; you shouldn't face this alone
  • Complementary practices like energy healing can help maintain strength between treatments
  • Remember: many patients feel this way, and most do complete treatment

After Treatment

I finished chemo but still feel exhausted. When will I feel normal?

You made it through treatment—that took real strength. But the exhaustion remains, and that's frustrating.

Here's what's happening: treatment is over, but your energy body is still recovering. Months of chemotherapy depleted your *prana*—your vital life force. Your physical body may be healing, but your energy system needs rebuilding too. This is why many survivors feel "flat" even when doctors say they're doing fine.

What helps:

  • Allow 6-12 months for full recovery; don't rush yourself
  • Gentle, regular movement helps restore energy flow
  • Prioritize sleep—this is when your body and energy regenerate
  • Practices like meditation, breathwork, or energy healing support prana restoration
  • Talk to someone about the emotional weight; it affects your energy too

"Normal" will come. It may be a new normal—often a stronger one.

Why do I feel anxious now that treatment is over?

This is surprisingly common, and you're not imagining it. Many survivors feel more anxious after treatment than during.

During treatment, you were taking action—fighting. Now, without that structure, anxiety can rush in. Fear of recurrence is real. Your nervous system has been in survival mode for months; it doesn't just switch off. And energetically, anxiety is often a sign of unprocessed fear stuck in your system.

What helps:

  • Acknowledge the anxiety; it's a normal response to what you've been through
  • Stay connected with your medical team; regular check-ups provide reassurance
  • Talk to someone—a counselor, support group, or trusted friend
  • Gentle practices like meditation and energy healing help release stored anxiety
  • Build a new routine; structure helps when life feels uncertain

How do I rebuild my strength after chemotherapy?

Rebuilding takes time, patience, and intention. Your body has been through a war; recovery isn't instant.

Chemotherapy affects every system—muscles, bones, organs, and your vital energy. Physical strength and energetic strength both need attention. Rebuilding *prana* is as important as rebuilding muscle.

What helps:

  • Start with gentle movement—walking, stretching, yoga—and build slowly
  • Eat protein-rich, nourishing foods to support tissue repair
  • Rest is not the enemy; recovery happens during rest
  • Energy healing and breathwork help restore your vital force
  • Set small, achievable goals; celebrate progress

Many survivors say they eventually feel stronger than before cancer. Give yourself time.

For Caregivers

I'm exhausted from caring for my family member. Is there help for me?

Yes. And thank you for asking—caregivers often forget to.

Caregiving is draining at every level: physical, emotional, and energetic. You're constantly giving your energy to someone else, and often there's nothing flowing back. In the language of ancient healing traditions, your *prana*—your life force—is being depleted. Without restoring it, burnout is inevitable.

What helps:

  • Take breaks, even 15 minutes; you cannot pour from an empty cup
  • Ask for and accept help with daily tasks
  • Maintain one activity that's just for you
  • Practices that rebuild prana—breathing exercises, time in nature, energy healing—are not luxuries; they're necessities
  • Connect with others who understand; isolation drains energy faster

You matter too. Your healing matters too.

How do I support my spouse through chemotherapy?

Being a supportive partner during cancer treatment is one of the hardest things you'll ever do. There's no perfect way to do it.

Your spouse's energy is depleted; they may not have much to give you. This can feel isolating. You're carrying fear, sadness, and practical burdens while trying to stay strong. Your own *prana* is being drained even as you try to support theirs.

What helps:

  • Be present without trying to fix everything; sometimes just being there is enough
  • Handle practical tasks so they can focus on healing
  • Don't suppress your own emotions; find someone to talk to
  • Take care of your own health; you can't help if you collapse
  • Accept that your relationship will change; it can become deeper

I feel guilty that I'm struggling when they're the one with cancer.

This guilt is so common among caregivers. Please hear this: your struggle is real and valid.

Caregiving is exhausting. Your fear, grief, and stress are not less real because you don't have cancer. You're allowed to struggle. In fact, acknowledging your struggle is the first step to getting the support you need—which ultimately makes you a better caregiver.

What helps:

  • Recognize that caregiver burnout is a real, documented phenomenon
  • Your feelings don't take anything away from your loved one's experience
  • Find a support group or counselor who understands caregiver stress
  • Taking care of yourself is not selfish; it's essential
  • You're doing something incredibly hard; be compassionate with yourself
