Viiveck Verma: A Caregiver's Journey

The caregiver needs care too. Viiveck took care of Prernaa, his wife. She had an 11-year journey with 9 reappearances of cancer. For both, it is a profile in courage.

The Beginning of an 11-Year Journey

When Prernaa was first diagnosed with cancer, we had no idea it would become an 11-year journey. Nine reappearances. Nine times we heard those words again. Nine times we had to find the strength to fight.

As her husband, I became her primary caregiver. It wasn't a role I choseâ€”it was a role that chose me. And like most caregivers, I was completely unprepared for what it would demand.

"The hardest part wasn't the medical appointments or the treatments. It was watching someone you love suffer and feeling helpless to stop it."

What Nobody Tells You About Being a Caregiver

Everyone focuses on the patient. And they shouldâ€”the patient is fighting for their life. But here's what nobody tells you: the caregiver is fighting too. Fighting exhaustion. Fighting fear. Fighting the guilt of having any needs of your own when your loved one is suffering.

There were nights I couldn't sleep, not because of any sound or disturbance, but because my mind wouldn't stop worrying. There were days when I had to be strong in front of Prernaa, then break down alone in the car afterward.

The emotional toll is invisible but immense:

  • Physical exhaustion from managing medications, appointments, and daily care
  • Emotional depletion from being the constant source of strength
  • Social isolation as life revolves around treatment schedules
  • Financial stress from medical expenses and reduced work capacity
  • Anticipatory grief that nobody wants to acknowledge

The Guilt of Self-Care

One of the most difficult things was accepting that I needed care too. How could I think about myself when Prernaa was the one with cancer? How could I feel tired when she was the one going through treatment?

But here's what I learned: You cannot pour from an empty cup.

When I finally allowed myself to seek supportâ€”whether it was talking to someone, taking a brief break, or simply acknowledging my own strugglesâ€”I became a better caregiver. I had more patience. More presence. More strength to give.

A Message to Caregivers

Your needs are not less important because someone else is sick. Taking care of yourself is not selfishâ€”it's necessary. You matter too.

What Helped Us Through

Over 11 years, we learned what made the journey more bearable:

1. Building a support network. We couldn't do it alone, and we shouldn't have tried. Friends, family, healthcare workers, support groupsâ€”everyone played a role.

2. Finding moments of normalcy. Even during treatment, we tried to maintain some routines. A cup of tea together. Watching a favorite show. Small moments of regular life amid the chaos.

3. Accepting help. This was hard for both of us. We were independent people. But learning to accept helpâ€”with meals, with errands, with just being thereâ€”made everything more manageable.

4. Talking about the hard things. We talked about fear. About death. About what we wanted. These conversations were painful but necessary. They brought us closer.

5. Complementary healing. Alongside medical treatment, we explored energy healing and aromatherapy. These didn't replace medicine, but they helped with side effects, stress, and emotional wellbeingâ€”for both of us.

Why I Co-Founded Anugnya

After Prernaa passed, I was lost. Thirty years of corporate leadership, mentoring 300+ startupsâ€”none of it prepared me for life after caregiving.

When I met Sangeeta and learned about her vision for Anugnya Holistic Care, I knew this was where I needed to be. Not despite my experience as a caregiver, but because of it.

I understand what caregivers go through. I understand the exhaustion, the isolation, the guilt. And I want to help others find the support that I wished I had.

At Anugnya, we don't just serve cancer patients. We serve caregivers too. Because the caregiver needs care too.

For Those Walking This Path Now

If you're a caregiver reading this, I want you to know:

You are not alone. Millions of people are walking this path alongside you, even if you can't see them.

Your feelings are valid. It's okay to feel scared, angry, sad, or exhausted. It's okay to have bad days.

Asking for help is strength. The strongest thing you can do is recognize when you need support and reach out for it.

This journey matters. What you're doingâ€”showing up every day for someone you loveâ€”is one of the most meaningful things a human being can do.

Prernaa's journey ended, but mine continues. And part of my journey now is making sure other caregivers know they don't have to walk alone.
